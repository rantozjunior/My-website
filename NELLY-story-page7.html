Chapter 4: Survivors And Truth

The tunnels stretched endlessly into darkness, echoing with the distant rumble of chaos from above. Beldarmore Valgra led the way, senses on high alert. Every shadow could hide a creature; every sound might be the prelude to another attack. Behind him, Heida clung to his arm, and Chiesniey’s rifle never left her shoulder. “Are we… the only ones down here?” Heida asked, voice trembling despite her attempts at composure. Beldarmore shook his head slightly, scanning the shadows. “I don’t think so. Somewhere down here, there might be more survivors. We just have to find them before whatever’s up there finds us again.” Chiesniey’s brow furrowed. “I don’t like this. We should move carefully. One wrong step, and…” She let the thought hang, unspoken..
.
.
Their progress was slow, deliberate, feet crunching over scattered debris, boots splashing in shallow puddles. The fluorescent lights overhead flickered sporadically, barely illuminating the graffiti-scarred walls. Then, up ahead, faint voices reached them. Whispered, hurried, and desperate. Beldarmore motioned for them to halt. “Stay quiet.” .
.
.
They crept toward the voices, emerging into a larger chamber filled with overturned benches and makeshift barricades. Huddled against the wall were several people, their faces pale and frightened, eyes wide at the sight of the newcomers. A young man with a ragged jacket spoke first. “You… you’re survivors too?” Beldarmore stepped forward, hands slightly raised, showing he meant no immediate harm. “Yes. We’re survivors. Name’s Beldarmore Valgra,” he said, voice calm but firm. “I’m here to make sure everyone gets out alive. You?” “Damon Helard,” the man said, voice shaky but trying to sound brave. Beside him, two others, Antonia Gladwell and a boy no older than sixteen, Heida recognized immediately, nodded silently. Heida glanced at them, heart tightening. “We… we can help each other,” she whispered..
.
.
Beldarmore’s protective instinct flared. He stepped slightly in front of Chiesniey and Heida, scanning the shadows around them. “No sudden movements,” he warned gently. “If anything comes, I’ll handle it.” Chiesniey’s rifle raised, but her eyes found Beldarmore’s. Unspoken, they acknowledged it: he was serious. Heida’s hand brushed against his, a faint reassurance of trust and need. Then the violet shimmer appeared. Xekara. She emerged from the darkness, her hair flowing like living smoke, eyes scanning every face in the chamber. Her presence was immediately commanding, almost hypnotic, and for a moment, every survivor froze. “Step back,” she said softly, but the tone was sharp enough to demand attention. “I am not your enemy. Yet.” Beldarmore stepped fully between her and the others, arms slightly raised, muscles tensed. “Who… what are you?” he demanded, voice steady. “And why should we trust you?” Xekara’s violet eyes met his. “Because if you do not listen, none of you will survive the night.” Chiesniey shifted slightly, rifle ready. “Explain. Now.” .
.
.
Xekara took a slow step forward, her voice calm, deliberate. “The rift… the sky tearing open, the creatures you have seen—they are not random. They are a force of change. The world you knew is ending. This is not an accident. It is a war… and you are in the middle of it.” Heida’s eyes widened. “A war? But… what do you mean? Who’s fighting?” Xekara’s gaze swept over the small group of survivors. “Not everyone who survives this will live. Some will serve the darkness. Some will resist. Those who survive… will choose their side. And you—” She paused, her violet eyes fixing on Beldarmore. “—have potential to decide more than you realize.” Beldarmore felt a shiver run down his spine. Protective instincts surged. “Enough riddles. Tell us what’s happening, or I swear—” “Enough threats,” Xekara interrupted, surprisingly soft. “Listen. These creatures are not random. They are drawn to fear, chaos, and life. The rift is a bridge, and it will grow unless it is closed.” Chiesniey’s sharp gaze narrowed. “And we’re supposed to just… stop it? How?”.
.
.
Xekara’s violet eyes flickered with something almost human—regret, maybe. “You cannot stop it alone. You will need power. You will need guidance. And you will need each other. Some of you… may not survive the lesson.” Beldarmore’s jaw tightened. He stepped closer to Chiesniey and Heida, placing himself protectively between them and Xekara. “I don’t care if you’re some mysterious cosmic force. You’re not touching them without my say-so. Understand?” Xekara tilted her head slightly, a faint, unreadable expression crossing her face. “I understand your protection… admirable. Foolish, perhaps. But admirable.” Heida’s small hand brushed against Beldarmore’s arm. “You… you’ll protect us, right?” .
.
.
His lips pressed together, jaw tightening. “I swear. No one will touch either of you while I’m standing.” Chiesniey’s voice was steady, but a flicker of warmth entered it. “We’ll watch each other’s backs. But Beldar… don’t do it alone.” Beldarmore nodded, the weight of responsibility settling heavily on him. They were all exhausted, all terrified, but he refused to let fear dominate them. Xekara moved closer, her voice dropping to almost a whisper. “Listen carefully. The rift’s expansion is not natural. It is drawn to life and to conflict. Your city—your world—is in its path. If you wish to survive, you must understand it, fight it, and… adapt. Some of you already have the potential.
